The Questions We Ask

Punishment and Restoration in US Justice System

While the Massachusetts RISE Program has been successful in providing an alternative to incarceration and a path to rehabilitation for some, restorative justice has a long way to go before becoming a viable solution. Resources are limited, skeptics are common and few of those who might be helped by restorative justice gets a chance to participate.

For everything from non-violent drug offenses to homicide, the U.S. justice system has one broad tool to address crime: incarceration. The U.S. prison population has thus ballooned exponentially. According to The Sentencing Project, there are 2 million people in U.S. prisons and jails, a 500% increase over the last 40 years. The U.S. accounts for 5% of the global population but more than 20% of the world's prison population, according to the American Civil Liberties Union.

Perhaps most disturbingly, even victims aren't satisfied with incarceration as a tool to address crime. According to the National Crime Victimization Survey, 52% of all violent victimizations were left unreported to the police between 2006 and 2019. The majority of respondents said they decided not to report the crime either because of a personal matter or because they wanted to deal with it another way.

“In the richest nation in the world, with arguably the largest and most expensive criminal justice system in human history, the majority of people who survive violence prefer nothing to everything we have to offer,” wrote Danielle Sered in her book, “Until We Reckon: Violence, Mass Incarceration, and a Road to Repair.” Sered is the founder of Common Justice, a restorative justice program in Brooklyn. “It is an extraordinary fact to absorb, and one that points to a moral crisis that is ours to answer to.”

“If you think about the way our criminal legal system currently works, there is little to no room within the structure of a court case for the victims,” said Susan Maze-Rothstein, the co-director of the Center for Restorative Justice at Suffolk University. “They are relegated to the role of witness. Arguably, [incarceration] is more retraumatizing because people are left with their harm and their pain.”

Criminal justice systems are defined by the questions they ask. In the United States, those questions often include, 'What laws have been broken?', 'Who did it?' and 'What do the offenders deserve?' For advocates like Sered, restorative justice is a way to reckon with and address problems with the criminal justice system. Though there are many variations on restorative justice, what they have in common is a focus on healing for victims and accountability by those who commit crimes, both to reckon with the harms they've caused and to take action to remedy the harms done to them that may have contributed to their actions.
