Predicting National Park Visitation Rates

Can the popularity of a given national park be correlated with its proxmity to the nearest MSR?


Introduction

The history of national parks in the United States dates back to 1872, when Yellowstone National Park was established as the first national park in the world. This was followed by the creation of several other national parks, such as Yosemite, Sequoia, and Mount Rainier, in the early 1900s. The National Park Service was established in 1916 to manage and protect the growing number of parks. Over time, more parks were added to the system, including the Grand Canyon, Acadia, and Zion, among others. Today, there are over 400 national parks and monuments in the United States.

The most popular national parks in the US today include Great Smoky Mountains National Park (which straddles North Carolina and Tennessee), Yellowstone National Park (which stretches across Wyoming, Montana, and Idaho), and Zion National Park (in southern Utah). Other highly visited parks include Rocky Mountain National Park (in Colorado), Grand Canyon National Park (in Arizona), and Yosemite National Park (in California).

Goals

My goal for this project is to examine whether there exists a correlation between the distance of national parks and metropolitan statistical areas. According to the Census Bureau, an MSA is defined as one or more counties that contain a city of 50,000 or more inhabitants, or contain a Census Bureau-defined urbanized area (UA) and have a total population of at least 100,000.

The main visualization will be a bivariate choropleth map that explores the relationship between the visitation rates at national parks and the distance to the nearest MSA.
