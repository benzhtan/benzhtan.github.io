Why are we scared of nuclear energy?

Subhead

By Benjamin ZH Tan

Confusion swam through Heather Hoff's mind as she sped through the darkness along the winding curves of the private access road that hugged the Pacific coast. To her right were the familiar rolling hills of California dotted with shrubbery. To her left, the inky black expanse of the Pacific Ocean. She had been urgently requested to report back to work before authorities closed access to the coastline stretching from Avila Beach in the south to Sonoma in the north.

"What's going on?" Hoff asked as she stepped into the briefing room of Diablo Canyon Nuclear Power Plant about 12 miles south-west of San Luis Obispo, California. "Tsunami watch," she was told. She and her fellow relief reactor operators were to go and watch the ocean for a rapidly receding tide, a harbinger of the chaos to come.

For Hoff, chaos began earlier that evening as she stood at her duty station in the reactor control room. The lone television in the briefing room informed the control room personnel of a large and powerful earthquake on the other side of the Pacific off the coast of Japan. The resulting tsunami washed ashore, flooding everything up to over six miles inland. Standing in the path of the formidable wall of water was Diablo Canyon's sister nuclear power plant: Fukushima-Daiichi.

"Those are my fellow nuclear operators that were terrified," said Hoff. "They don't know if their families were okay and their coworkers were around the plant. They don't know if people are dead. They don't have any indications of what's going on. It was a nightmare scenario."

The nightmare was just beginning. As the media coverage of the unfolding calamity intensified, misinformation began to spread. Nuclear industry insiders, like Hoff, had access to more information than the general public did. But that did not prevent rumors from spreading that a burning spent fuel pool indicated that an explosion at Fukushima was imminent.

"The Chernobyl accident was bad because the core itself exploded and distributed [debris]," Hoff said, explaining why the Soviet government evacuated the nearby city of Pripyat when a reactor exploded at the Chernobyl Nuclear Power Plant in April 1986. "So if the fuel pools burned or exploded, that would be kind of a similar situation. I'm terrified. I'm like, 'it's the next Chernobyl and there's three of them.'"

Ultimately, not only was the rumor proven to be false, it turned out that it was perpetuated by the former Commissioner of the Nuclear Regulatory Commission, Gregory Jaczko.

"And (the media) just ran with it," Hoff said.


Since the Fukushima accident, climate change has come to the forefront of the public consciousness. According to the World Meteorological Organization, eight of the warmest in recorded history have occurred since 2015. In 2023, a report published by the United Nations Conference of the Parties suggested that humanity's window to limit global warming to 1.5ÂºC might be closing more rapidly than previously thought. Calls to decarbonize the energy grid have intensified but frequently omit nuclear energy because of decades of negative media coverage that formed negative connotations of nuclear energy in the public consciousness.

When nuclear energy made its debut in the 1950s, it was heralded as a revolutionary source of electricity that was going to be "too cheap to meter," as the saying went. But nuclear energy's destructive origins were never far from mind. The Three Mile Island accident coincided almost perfectly with the theatrical release of "The China Syndrome," which only reinforced the public perception of the dangers inherent to nuclear energy.

Hoff, who founded a pro-nuclear advocacy group called Mothers for Nuclear, frequently takes straw polls during speaking engagements to encourage people to share their perception of nuclear energy.

"We love to do that because it gives people a chance to really kind of air their dirty laundry," Hoff explained. "Like 'shout out all the crazy!' The glowing green goo, the explosions, weapons, waste, three-eyed fish. Every once in a while we get some people that are like, 'clean power!' and we're like, 'yay!' But not always."

The disconnect between nuclear energy and clean power is reflected in how the news media covers nuclear energy.

An analysis of articles that appeared in the New York Times from 2010 to 2022 shows the evolution of how the news media reports on nuclear energy. The news media's coverage of nuclear energy can largely be broken down into three sections. Nuclear energy in the context of the "nuclear industrial complex," nuclear energy in the context of the Fukushima disaster, and nuclear energy in the context of climate change.

As can be expected, coverage in 2011 is focused largely on nuclear, though there is also considerable attention given to energy and oil. Given the events at the Fukushima-Daiichi Nuclear Power Plant, Coverage of nuclear energy predictably became sadder, more negative, and more fearful.

Once immediate fears of a meltdown subsided, news media coverage of nuclear energy turned into disgust in 2012, focusing largely on the clean up from the accident in the previous year. In addition to the distinct cluster surrounding "nuclear," radioactivity is a recurrent topic. Storage issues take on more prominence, with mentions of "interim storage," "permanent repository," and Yucca Mountain.

After two years of focusing on the Fukushima disaster, the news media turned its attention to the nuclear industry within the US. Much of the discussion about nuclear revolved around the nuclear power plants, the Nuclear Regulatory Commission, and public opinion. Energy also dominated news coverage in 2013, addressing renewables, policy, and new technology.

One of these new clean energy technologies is solar. Solar gained prominence in 2014, especially around solar panel ownership and installation. Nuclear power is extensively discussed in the context of regulation, and energy security is emphasized. Commonly appearing bigrams include phrases such as "disaster," "contingency," and "China Syndrome," a 1979 disaster film whose plot hinges around safety cover ups at a fictitious nuclear power plant.

Climate change became more prominent in 2015 than in previous years due, in large part, to the Paris Agreement, a legally binding international treaty created to address climate change mitigation, adaptation, and financing. Notably, signatories agreed to limit global warming to 1.5ÂºC by adopting net-zero emission policies. News media coverage focuses predominantly on a low emissions energy mix featuring solar, wind, and wave energy.

While there is a strong correlation between nuclear and power in 2015, there is correlation to cooperation and weapons, which point towards the agreement reached on the Joint Comprehensive Plan of Action.

Coverage of nuclear in 2016 occurs in the context of energy and gas, specifically, clean and renewable energy, and natural gas. Wind also features prominently, particularly coverage of onshore wind projects. Despite the positive lean of coverage in 2016, there is some residual disgust from the release of radioactive water from Fukushima-Daiichi. An ice wall was built around the facility to prevent groundwater from flowing through the plant on its way to the ocean.

The debate between nuclear power and natural gas intensified in 2017. Proponents of the natural gas industry point to nuclear's high implementation costs and argue in favor of investing in natural gas, which is a cheaper form of energy, rather than expanding existing nuclear plants.

Nuclear energy is discussed within the broader framework of an energy mix in 2018. Although renewable sources such solar and wind are favored, the Trump Administration chooses to subsidize the struggling coal industry. Moreover, the energy grid, which is considered critical infrastructure, was found to be vulnerable to Russian hackers.

2019 is a significant year for the energy industry in Europe. Although countries like Germany and France have a strong history of souring their electricity from nuclear, the Nord Stream 2 pipeline carrying liquified natural gas from Russia to Germany is aggressively promoted. European governments see this as an important development because cheap Russian natural gas help keep electricity prices low.

While reducing carbon emissions takes on new importance in 2020, the energy industry is hit hard by the Covid-19 pandemic. Major energy companies such as Exxon Mobile, Chervon, and Shell receive loans from the government to help them weather the worst of the pandemic.

Various forms of clean energy are discussed in 2021, highlighting the transition to a more balanced energy mix.

In 2022, nuclear energy is closely related with clean energy. While start ups are coming online, the nuclear's sentiment is darkened by cost overruns and concerns over radioactive waste. An energy crisis in Europe is frequently discussed in light of the ongoing hostilities between Ukraine and Russia which threaten to cut off supplies of Russian natural gas to Germany.
